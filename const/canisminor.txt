Canis Minor is derived from the Latin language and means “the lesser or smaller dog.” It represents the smaller dog following the hunter Orion. It can be found in the second quadrant of the Northern hemisphere and is located specifically between latitudes of +90° and -75°. Along with other constellations, Canis Minor was first catalogued in the 2nd century by the Greek astronomer, Ptolemy.
main stars
Procyon (Alpha Canis Minoris) shines the brightest in the Canis Minor constellation (which will be discussed later in detail).
Gomeisa (Beta Canis Minoris) shines the second brightest in Canis Minor and is classified as a Gamma Cassiopeiae variable; rotates quickly and has variations in its luminosity.
Gamma Canis Minoris is a double star with the main one being an orange K-type giant. It is located 398 light-years away.
G Canis Minoris is a binary star with another orange K-type giant as its main component.
Luyten’s Star (GJ 273) is hard to observe as it is a very faint red dwarf that is located 12.36 light-years away. It is the 22nd nearest star system.
